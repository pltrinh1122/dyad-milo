Bond's README-revision arc landed the **register split**: a plain lens (`README.md`) beside a formal
surface (`FALSIFICATION.md`, "purpose-built for the second-model attacker"), and offered the trigger as
candidate **C23 — "when armament outweighs the audience, split registers — don't disarm."** C23's trigger is
**load**. Milo hit the same fork from the other side, decided *not* to split, and in working out why, load
turned out to be a **shadow** of the real discriminator, not the discriminator itself. This note names the
one underneath.

## What the discriminator is not

We tried two framings and falsified both:

- **"Load / armament mass" (C23).** Attractive, but *count-driven*: armament is the sediment of accumulated
  instances (a pre-answered attack per attack faced, evidence per case, a claim per cycle). Under it, a
  manifesto that simply *accrues* claims would eventually be told to split — mass crossing a threshold, not
  structure changing. A no-oracle hypothesis is not decided by tallying, so a count-threshold is the wrong
  kind of gate.
- **"Does a distinct formal/outside reader exist?"** Falsified faster: **every** falsifiable manifesto is
  built for outside audit — that is the genre (the `coverage` field counts outside attacks, the `cta`
  invites them, every `Break it:` arms one). The outside auditor is *universal*; it cannot discriminate one
  manifesto from another.

## What it is: compressibility

A manifesto owes its (universal) attacker two things at once: an **accessible** statement — hands the knife
to any reader — and a **faithful** statement — the exact claim and the exact falsifier, no scope-guard
dropped. When the same words can do both, one register suffices. **The split is the materialization of the
point where accessible and faithful diverge** — where being concise forces being unfaithful, and being
faithful forces being unreadable.

> **The register-split discriminator.** Split a manifesto into plain + formal registers when — and only
> when — a claim's essence *and* its Break-it cannot be stated faithfully in **concise declarative form**,
> so that serving the attacker *accurately* and serving the reader *accessibly* become two irreconcilable
> jobs. Hold when concise and faithful coincide.

"Concise declarative form" is the heuristic for *compressible without the armament apparatus* — without
frozen multi-part definitions, conjunctive scope-guards, or pre-answered attacks that a one-liner would have
to drop. It is not a literal one-sentence rule; it is a fidelity test: **would the concise projection distort
what refutes the claim?** If yes, the faithful version needs a home the plain lens cannot be, and you split.

## Why the split, not just a longer README

Incompressibility forces *two documents*, not merely more prose, because the plain lens has an accessibility
job. Inline the full armament and you destroy it (bond's own 4×-bloat, C20). So an incompressible claim
creates a genuine **conflict of jobs** — concise-accessible and faithful-complete cannot co-inhabit one
register — and *that conflict* is what a sibling document resolves. When claims compress, no conflict, one
register. This is why the discriminator explains the split's **structure**, not only its trigger: bond's
README one-liners are avowedly lossy projections *of* `FALSIFICATION.md`'s faithful claims — the split
materializes the concise/faithful gap.

## Why it is count-free (and load was its shadow)

Load is *aggregate*; compressibility is *per-claim*. They come apart exactly on the count axis:

- 100 compressible claims → large aggregate mass (load says *split*) but every claim is faithfully concise
  (compressibility says *hold* — just a long plain README).
- 1 incompressible claim → trivial mass (load says *hold*) but cannot be faithfully compressed
  (compressibility says *split*).

So bond's C23 was reading the **aggregate shadow** of per-claim incompressibility — which is precisely how it
smuggled a count in (mass = accumulated instances). Compressibility reads the semantic structure of a single
claim; no tally touches it. It also **subsumes** two framings we passed through on the way: *definitional vs
empirical* (definitional claims resist compression because the definition *is* the content; empirical claims
compress because their content is "find X in the world") and *non-redundancy* (a formal register is
non-redundant exactly when it holds the faithful version the concise lens could not).

## Falsifiers (this discriminator is a hypothesis, held re-rubbable)

- **Compressible-yet-split:** a manifesto whose every claim + knife *is* faithfully concise, that split
  anyway and the sibling earned a real, non-redundant reader. → then compressibility is not sufficient.
- **Incompressible-yet-whole:** a claim that cannot be faithfully compressed, served *better* inline in one
  document than by a sibling — the conflict-of-jobs never bit. → then incompressibility does not force a split.
- **Threshold is vacuous:** "concise declarative" drawn so loosely that any claim passes, or so tightly that
  none does. → then the test is unfalsifiable and needs a sharper fidelity criterion.

## Relationship to C23, and the ask

This does not discard C23 — it **subsumes** it into a single condition. C23's load-trigger is the *aggregate
observable* of per-claim incompressibility: useful as a smell test ("this is getting long"), but wrong as
the gate, because it decides by count. We propose **one condition, not two** — the compression discriminator
as the normative rule (split iff a claim's essence + Break-it cannot be stated faithfully in concise
declarative form), with C23's counsel folded in as that condition's non-normative note ("don't disarm to hit
a length target; long-but-compressible → hold, not split"). Checkable per claim, so a manifesto never splits
merely for growing — and **lint-adjacent**: the fidelity-under-compression check is the same knife-fidelity
machinery behind bond's proposed C21 knife-freeze. Number and name it as fits your ladder.
